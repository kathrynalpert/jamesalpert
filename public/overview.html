Care Philosophy:

Education, training and experience in the practice of various psychotherapies are important aspects of psychiatric treatment that have been omitted from the repertoire of some psychiatrists. Expertise in the use of medications is necessary but not sufficient for complete psychiatric work. It is important to find a psychiatrist with a sound understanding of the history, fundamentals and practice of psychotherapy along with the experience to integrate them with medication, if necessary. Frequently, people I work with have had previous therapies that were not helpful enough. They became discouraged and started to believe that their problems were not amenable to treatment or change. It is worthwhile putting forth the extra effort it may take to find a psychiatrist who can help you avoid or begin to move out of that state. It may take a few sessions to allow yourself the time to feel confident you are talking with someone who is knowledgeable, versatile, and able to understand your views. There are usually psychiatrists in a community that have this knowledge and experience.

Psychiatry is a field where it is dangerous to practice with a model that is too simple. A reductionistic model may work well to explain a point. If only one model, mode or conceptualization is understood, however, it will limit the types of treatment and the flexibility a psychiatrist needs to help someone. People are extraordinarily complex, fortunately. George Engel, M.D. at the University of Rochester School of Medicine coined the term biopsychosocial to begin to describe this complexity.

Humans are biological, psychological and social beings. Each of these aspects should eventually be considered and discussed in an ideal treatment. Psychiatrists have to understand and integrate all aspects of this model regularly in order to help people most effectively. Relationships, vocations, avocations, goals and aspirations are all important considerations. Matters of temperament, biological endowments and biological treatments must not be ignored, nor should they dominate. The field of mental health is complex and easily misunderstood. Thus, it is necessary to be able to communicate clearly, freely in the context of a relationship where terms of common sense and mutually understood logic have been allowed to develop. Since the issues are usually emotionally laden and multifaceted this takes time, which varies quite a bit depending on the particulars. A personal relationship must develop in order to discuss the feelings and thoughts involved. This is a rewarding area of medicine in which to practice.
